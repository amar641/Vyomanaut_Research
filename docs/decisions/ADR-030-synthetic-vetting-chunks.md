# ADR-030 — Synthetic Vetting Chunks: Repair-Safe Provider Assessment

**Status:** Accepted
**Topic:** #5 Peer Selection (vetting mechanism)**Supersedes:** ADR-005 §Subsystem 2 (vetting chunk assignment policy)
**Superseded by:** —
**Research source:** ADR-003, ADR-004, ADR-005, ADR-010

---

## Context

ADR-005 established a four-subsystem reputation pipeline in which vetting providers receive real file shards under extra erasure redundancy during their 4–6 month assessment period. This creates a structural coupling between the vetting system and the repair system: when a vetting provider departs — the event the vetting period exists to screen for — the repair scheduler enqueues jobs for real data owner files.

At N=1,000 the Qpeek formula (ADR-004, Giroire Formula 2) yields ~793 GB of burst transfer per departure. For ACTIVE providers who have earned trust over 80+ consecutive audit passes, this cost is accepted: their departure is anomalous and the data must be protected. For vetting providers, departure is statistically expected — the vetting period *is*the filter. Spending 793 GB of repair bandwidth to discover that a provider leaves is the opposite of efficient assessment.

At V2 launch, N will be near its minimum (56–200 providers). BWavg scales as D/N; at small N the absolute repair bandwidth per departure consumes a disproportionate share of the 100 Kbps background budget. The network is most fragile precisely when vetting departures are most frequent (early adopters are least committed).

The additional concern identified in ADR-005 open constraints: placing real data owner shards on unvetted providers weakens the durability guarantee during the vetting window, even with extra erasure redundancy, because the extra redundancy is itself placed on similarly unvetted providers.

## Options Considered

| Option | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Status quo (real shards on vetting providers, extra redundancy) | No schema change; tests real upload path | Repair triggered on every vetting departure; degrades BWavg during most fragile phase; data owner shards on unproven nodes |
| Separate vetting cluster (vetting providers never receive real assignments) | Zero repair risk during vetting | Providers pass vetting on a different workload than production; behavioural mismatch; operational complexity of two track types |
| **Synthetic vetting chunks (chosen)** | Zero repair cost on departure; identical audit path to production; no data owner data at risk; clean GC on ACTIVE transition | Providers do not handle real data during vetting; real upload throughput to vetting providers is untested |

## Decision

During the vetting period, providers receive only **synthetic vetting chunks** — random 256 KB blocks generated by the microservice — rather than real file shards.

### What is identical between synthetic and real chunks

- Storage path: the provider daemon writes the chunk to the vLog and the RocksDB index using the identical `AppendChunk` code path. The daemon cannot distinguish synthetic from real.
- Audit path: the microservice issues `ChallengeRequest` messages identically. The provider reads from the vLog, verifies `content_hash`, computes `SHA-256(chunk_data || nonce)`, and returns a signed receipt. Identical in every respect.
- Payment path: the provider earns one audit-pass credit per synthetic chunk per day. The escrow ledger records these under the standard `RELEASE` event type. Synthetic chunks generate real earnings during vetting, which is correct: the vetting period is testing reliability under normal incentive conditions.
- Scoring path: `consecutive_audit_passes` increments identically; the VETTING → ACTIVE transition fires on the 80th consecutive pass regardless of chunk type.

### What differs

- `chunk_assignments.is_vetting_chunk = TRUE` for synthetic assignments.
- `chunk_assignments.segment_id` is `NULL` for synthetic assignments (no real file).
- `audit_receipts.file_id` is `NULL` for audits of synthetic chunks (no real file owner).
- The repair scheduler checks `is_vetting_chunk` before enqueueing jobs. A vetting provider departure enqueues **zero**repair jobs. The synthetic chunk data is discarded.
- The assignment service caps synthetic chunk allocation at **10% of `declared_storage_gb`** for vetting providers (see §Storage cap below).
- On ACTIVE transition, the daemon receives a GC instruction to delete all its vetting chunks. Only after GC completes does the assignment service begin queuing real shard assignments.

### Synthetic chunk generation

The microservice generates synthetic chunk data on the fly:

```go
syntheticData := make([]byte, 262144) // 256 KB
_, err := crypto/rand.Read(syntheticData)
chunkID := sha256.Sum256(syntheticData)
```

The `chunkID` (SHA-256 of the random data) is stored in `chunk_assignments.chunk_id` and transmitted to the provider daemon in the standard chunk upload stream (`/vyomanaut/chunk-upload/1.0.0`). The provider stores it, verifies `SHA-256(syntheticData) == chunkID`, and writes it to the vLog. From the daemon's perspective this is indistinguishable from a real shard.

The synthetic data itself does not need to be retained by the microservice after the upload is confirmed. The `chunk_id`(SHA-256) in `chunk_assignments` is the only record needed to issue future audit challenges: the microservice generates a fresh challenge nonce and expects the provider to prove they still hold the data by returning `SHA-256(syntheticData || nonce)`. The microservice cannot verify the response hash directly (same as real chunks — it never holds the chunk data). The Ed25519 signature verification and the economic deterrence of score penalties together remain the validation mechanism.

### Storage cap

During vetting, the assignment service enforces:

```
max_vetting_chunks = floor(declared_storage_gb × 1024 × 1024 / 262144 × 0.10)
                   = floor(declared_storage_gb × 400)
```

A provider declaring 100 GB receives at most 40,000 synthetic chunks (~10 GB). A provider declaring 1,000 GB receives at most 400,000 synthetic chunks (~100 GB).

The cap is enforced at assignment time by querying the current active vetting chunk count:

```sql
SELECT COUNT(*)
FROM chunk_assignments
WHERE provider_id = $1
  AND is_vetting_chunk = TRUE
  AND status = 'ACTIVE';
```

If this count equals `max_vetting_chunks`, no further synthetic assignments are made until some are retired. The cap exists so a vetting provider's storage is not fully committed before they are trusted with real data. After ACTIVE, real shard assignments ramp to the full `declared_storage_gb` limit as file uploads arrive.

### ACTIVE transition sequence

When `consecutive_audit_passes` reaches 80 AND `NOW() - first_chunk_assignment_at >= 120 days`:

1. `providers.status` is set to `'ACTIVE'` in the same database transaction.
2. The assignment service sends a GC instruction to the provider daemon: a list of all `chunk_id` values where `is_vetting_chunk = TRUE AND provider_id = $1 AND status = 'ACTIVE'`.
3. The daemon deletes these chunks from the vLog (using the standard `DeleteChunk` + GC path).
4. The assignment service sets those `chunk_assignments.status = 'DELETED'`.
5. The assignment service begins queuing real shard assignments.

Steps 3–5 are asynchronous: the provider becomes ACTIVE immediately (step 1); real assignments begin flowing while GC runs in the background.

### New libp2p sub-protocol for vetting GC

A new microservice-to-daemon protocol delivers the GC instruction list:

```
Protocol ID: /vyomanaut/vetting-gc/1.0.0
```

The microservice sends a list of `chunk_id` values (32 bytes each, length-prefixed). The daemon calls `DeleteChunk` for each and responds with a per-chunk success/failure bitmap.

This protocol uses the same 0-RTT prohibition as audit challenges (the instruction carries financial consequence — incorrect GC of real chunks would be catastrophic). In practice, a vetting GC stream will never carry real chunk IDs since real chunks are never assigned to vetting providers; the prohibition is a defensive policy.

## Consequences

**Positive:**

- Zero repair bandwidth cost on vetting provider departure. The network's most fragile phase (small N, frequent early-adopter churn) consumes no repair bandwidth for expected events.
- No data owner data is placed on unproven providers at any point in V2.
- The audit path is genuinely tested: providers experience identical storage and challenge mechanics as production. Behavioural differences between synthetic and real chunks do not exist at the daemon level.
- The 10% storage cap ensures vetting providers experience realistic, meaningful storage load without monopolising their disk for dummy data.

**Negative / trade-offs:**

- The real upload throughput path (data owner → provider chunk upload) is not exercised during vetting. The first real shard upload a provider handles is after they are ACTIVE. This is acceptable: the audit challenge path (which tests storage and retrieval fidelity) is exercised daily; upload throughput is measured during that path via `response_latency_ms`.
- The microservice must generate and transmit 256 KB random blocks per synthetic assignment. At 56 vetting providers each assigned 10,000 synthetic chunks (a moderate scenario), this is ~146 GB of one-time transfer at the start of vetting. This is a launch-time cost, not steady-state. It is bounded by the 10% cap and spread over the assignment ramp-up period.
- Synthetic chunks introduce a new `NULL`able `segment_id` in `chunk_assignments` and `file_id` in `audit_receipts`. The CHECK constraints in `data-model.md` enforce that these nulls are only present when `is_vetting_chunk = TRUE`, preventing accidental null propagation to real chunks.

**Open constraints:**

- The vetting GC instruction delivery (step 2 of the ACTIVE transition) is best-effort at the time of transition: if the provider is offline, the instruction is queued and retried on the next heartbeat connection. Until the provider comes back online and completes GC, the old vetting chunk rows remain in `status = 'ACTIVE'` but no further challenges are issued for them (the audit scheduler checks `status = 'ACTIVE'` — transitioning them to `'PENDING_DELETION'` on ACTIVE-transition is the correct mechanism; see data-model.md).
- Pricing for vetting chunk storage cost to the microservice operator (disk on provider machines is real) is covered by the standard per-audit-pass payout. The 10% cap keeps this reasonable.
